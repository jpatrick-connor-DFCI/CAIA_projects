Test-set discrimination (held-out C-index and time-dependent AUC) is shown below — a longer landmark window means more lab observations are available, but at the cost of conditioning the survival population on having survived past the landmark.

What the trends say: For the death endpoint, predictability improves monotonically from baseline (0.71) to +60d (0.76) and plateaus at +90d (0.75) — adding the post-index lab trajectory clearly helps. 3. PSA & Testosterone — Signal Strength vs. Landmark Time

For each landmark, we plot the −log10(q-value) of every PSA / Testosterone summary statistic in the unadjusted univariable Cox model. Higher = stronger statistical signal after Benjamini–Hochberg correction. Marker shape encodes direction: ▲ = HR > 1 (higher value → higher hazard), ▼ = HR < 1 (protective).

Coverage of PSA & Testosterone over time

Fraction of patients with at least one observed value contributing to each summary statistic. Coverage growth with landmark window is the principal mechanism by which these labs gain power.

Key observations on PSA:
  • PSA n_observations (count of PSA draws in the window) is consistently the most significant PSA-related feature for both endpoints — a clear surveillance/sickness-frequency signal (HR ≈ 1.27–1.34, q < 1e-13 by +30d).
  • Higher PSA value statistics (mean, last, max) are protective in the univariable models (HR < 1) — only at later landmarks. At baseline they are not significant. By +90d, mean PSA reaches HR=0.49, q=3e-08 for death.
  • The protective-PSA signal is most likely confounded by treatment response: patients with measurable PSA dynamics are those alive long enough to have several lab draws and on therapy; those who die fast contribute mostly to n_observations, not to value-summaries.
Key observations on Testosterone:
  • Testosterone min and mean are HR > 1 across every landmark for both endpoints — higher testosterone predicts worse outcomes.
  • Strongest unadjusted effect: Testosterone min HR=1.39 (q=5e-16) at +60d for death; Testosterone mean HR=1.50 (q=3e-07) at +60d for platinum.
  • Interpretation: in a heavily ADT-treated metastatic population, failure to suppress testosterone — or testosterone recovery — is a marker of either non-castrate disease, treatment non-adherence, or progressive disease. This is the inverse of the classical "low T = bad" framing in healthy populations.
  • Testosterone slope and delta have low coverage (0.21–0.69) and contribute little signal — most patients lack the longitudinal density required for meaningful trajectory features.
